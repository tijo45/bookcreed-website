# 5 Signs You're Addicted to Political Intrigue Fantasy (And What to Read Next)

You don't read fantasy for the battles.

The battles are fine. The battles are great, even. But what you're *really* there for is the scene before the battle — the one where two people who are supposedly on the same side have a polite conversation and you realize, with growing dread, that one of them is absolutely about to destroy the other. Metaphorically. Strategically. With a smile.

If you find yourself skimming the action sequences to get back to the court intrigue, this list is for you. Here are five signs you've developed a full-blown political intrigue fantasy habit — and some suggestions for what to do about it.

---

## Sign #1: You Analyze Every Character's Motive Like It's a Job Interview

Normal readers: *Oh, interesting, a new character.*

You: *Why are they here? Who sent them? What do they want that they're not saying? What do they want that they don't even know they want? Are they already compromised?*

You've started treating fictional allies like hostile witnesses. You maintain a running internal spreadsheet of who owes what to whom, which debts are likely to be called in, and which characters are pretending to be less powerful than they are. You've been burned before. You're not getting burned again.

**What to read:** *The Lies of Locke Lamora* by Scott Lynch is custom-built for this kind of reader. Locke Lamora is a con man operating in a city where everyone is running a game inside someone else's game — and Lynch layers the deceptions so carefully that you genuinely cannot tell when you're being played until the moment he wants you to find out. Warning: your motive-analysis skills will be pushed to their limits, and they will still fail you.

---

## Sign #2: You've Lost Sleep Wondering Who's Really in Charge

The nominal ruler is on the throne. That much is clear.

But who actually controls the information reaching them? Who has leverage over the people who control the information? Who put those people in place, and what did it cost? Is the ruler a knowing participant in the machinery of power, or a comfortable figurehead? And if it's the latter — does *the ruler know that*?

These are not questions you ask at 2 a.m. voluntarily. They are questions that your brain asks because you have been reading books that rewired your relationship to authority.

**What to read:** *Dune* by Frank Herbert is the canonical text for this affliction. Herbert builds a universe where the official power structures are almost entirely decorative — the real control lies in who controls the spice, who controls information, and who has the patience to play across generational timescales. The Emperor is powerful. The Spacing Guild is more powerful. The Bene Gesserit have been running a breeding program for centuries and are playing a game whose end goal most of the characters can barely conceptualize. *Dune* is the book that gave an entire generation of readers the habit of asking "but who's *really* in charge?" about everything, including real life. You were warned.

---

## Sign #3: You've Drawn a Faction Diagram on Paper You Had to Throw Away

It started innocently. You just wanted to track who was allied with whom. A few circles, some lines, a couple of color codes for relationship types.

Two hours later you had a document spanning three pages that included sub-factions, historical grievances, and a footnote about a marriage that happened forty years before the story takes place that is definitely going to matter.

You threw it away because if anyone found it, you couldn't explain it.

**What to read:** *Mistborn: The Final Empire* by Brandon Sanderson for the satisfaction of a scheme that actually pays off. Sanderson is the master of the long con — he lays out a plan, you watch the plan fail spectacularly, and then you realize the failure was part of a larger plan you didn't even know existed. The intrigue in Mistborn has mechanical elegance: every piece exists for a reason, and the payoff is proportional to how carefully you were paying attention. For readers who want their faction diagrams to eventually resolve into something coherent, this is your book.

---

## Sign #4: Every Betrayal Hits You Like a Personal Affront

You didn't just see it coming — you *suspected* it, dismissed the suspicion as too cynical, talked yourself back into trust, and then got hit anyway.

The betrayal in *Red Rising* by Pierce Brown. The moment in *A Game of Thrones* you're thinking of right now. The scene in *The Lies of Locke Lamora* that you cannot talk about without your voice going flat and strange. These aren't just plot events for you. They're formative experiences. You have opinions about fictional characters who don't exist that are more visceral than your feelings about most news events.

This is because good political intrigue fiction makes you believe in the relationships before it destroys them. You've been made to trust, which means you've been made to feel the cost of misplaced trust in a way that purely action-driven narratives don't attempt.

**What to read:** *Red Rising* by Pierce Brown is essentially a masterclass in manufactured trust and strategic betrayal. Pierce builds alliances, lets you root for them, then shows you exactly how fragile they were — and then, in the sequels, asks whether people can choose something better when they know how the game is played. It's brutal and hopeful in roughly equal measure, which is rarer than you'd think.

---

## Sign #5: You're More Excited by Dialogue Than Combat

The fight scene is impressive. You appreciate the craft. But you were *vibrating* during the negotiation scene three chapters earlier, when two characters were talking around something neither of them would name directly, and the entire balance of power in the story shifted in a three-minute conversation with no violence at all.

That's the scene you'll be thinking about later. That's the scene you'll quote to your friends who also read this kind of thing. The sword fight resolved a conflict; the dialogue *created* about six new ones.

**What to read:** *The Goblin Emperor* by Katherine Addison for a counterintuitive take — a protagonist who wants nothing to do with intrigue being thrown into the deep end of it and having to learn the language of court politics while remaining genuinely good. Addison writes political maneuvering as a form of character expression: how people use language to reveal and conceal themselves, how power flows through the gaps between words. If you love what a carefully written conversation can do, this book will reward you enormously.

---

## What to Read When You've Finished All of These

If you've burned through everything on this list and you're looking for something that takes political intrigue seriously without softening the edges — backstabbing courts, characters who say one thing and mean another, power structures that punish loyalty and reward patience — the [Kingdom of Valdrath series](https://bookcreed.com/books/the-exiles-return) might be exactly what you need.

[*The Exile's Return*](https://www.amazon.com/dp/B0GKXNCCXD) drops an exiled prince into the middle of a murder investigation at the court that cast him out. Everyone has something to hide. Most of them are very good at hiding it. The antagonist is charismatic, politically sophisticated, and operating from motives that are genuinely difficult to dismiss — which is the only kind of antagonist worth caring about.

If you've made it through five signs and you're still nodding: this is your next read.
