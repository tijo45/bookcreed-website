# Writing Combat Scenes That Matter: Lessons from Eight Books of Bloodshed

I've written hundreds of fight scenes across eight books. Sword fights, ambushes, sieges, duels, assassinations, one-on-one confrontations, and a full-scale war involving a hundred thousand troops. I've choreographed so much fictional violence that I dream in parry-riposte sequences.

Here's what I've learned: **the choreography is the least important part.**

If you're writing combat and spending most of your energy on the physical mechanics — which foot goes where, how the blade angles, the precise trajectory of the counterstrike — you're building the wrong thing. You're decorating a house with no foundation.

Fight scenes that matter aren't about the fight. They're about what the fight *reveals*.

Let me show you what I mean.

## Lesson 1: Every Fight Is a Character Scene in Disguise

The most important fight in *The Exile's Return* isn't a surprise attack or a battle sequence. It's the Challenge Right between Cassian and Lucian — two brothers fighting in the Grand Arena with ten thousand people watching and the fate of a kingdom hanging on the outcome.

The choreography matters. I worked hard on it. But what makes the scene *land* is everything that isn't choreography:

- **What Cassian notices** during the fight tells you who he is. He reads Lucian's footwork the way a therapist reads body language. He sees the rage, the desperation, the technique drilled into them both by the same trainers. He's not just fighting his brother — he's reading him.
- **What Cassian chooses** when he wins is the real climax. He has every right to kill Lucian. The Code permits it. The crowd expects it. And he chooses exile. That choice — made in the heat of combat, standing over a defeated opponent — tells you more about Cassian than ten chapters of introspection.
- **What the crowd does** shows you the kingdom. Their reaction to Cassian's mercy — the confusion, the disappointment, the grudging respect from some and outright disgust from others — reveals the culture these characters live in.

The actual swordwork? Important for pacing, for visceral engagement, for making the reader feel the impact. But it's the *servant* of the character work, not the master.

**Practical takeaway:** Before writing any fight scene, answer this question: *What does this fight reveal about the characters that couldn't be revealed any other way?* If the answer is "nothing," the fight is decorative. Cut it or restructure it until it's earning its place.

## Lesson 2: Stakes Aren't Just Physical

"Will the character survive?" is the least interesting question a fight scene can ask. Of course they'll survive — they're the protagonist, and we're on page 200 of an 800-page book. We know the math.

The interesting stakes are internal:

- **Will Cassian keep his oath to never kill again?** When he fights, the tension isn't about whether his opponent's blade will find his throat. It's about whether the violence will pull him back into being the person he was — the prince who executed innocent people because a king told him to.
- **Will Theo prove himself?** When the youngest Stormborn prince faces his Rite of Scars at seventeen, during a succession crisis, the question isn't whether he'll survive seven bouts. It's whether he'll emerge as someone the kingdom can follow. Physical survival is the minimum. The real test is transformation.
- **Will mercy survive contact with reality?** This is the macro-stake of the entire series. Cassian's philosophy of mercy is tested every time violence finds him. Each fight asks: can you be merciful and effective? Can you refuse to become a monster without being destroyed by them?

**Practical takeaway:** Layer your stakes. Every fight should have at least two levels — the physical outcome (who wins) and the thematic outcome (what it means). The best fights have three or four layers running simultaneously.

## Lesson 3: Pacing Is Music, Not Mathematics

New writers often pace fight scenes mechanically: action, action, action, pause, action, action, climax. It reads like a metronome. Technically correct, emotionally flat.

Fight scenes need *rhythm*, and rhythm means variation.

In the final duel between Cassian and Lucian in Book 6 — the fight I spent more time on than any other in the series — the pacing does something specific. The physical combat is fast, brutal, and relatively short. But I slow time inside Cassian's head at key moments:

- When Lucian's blade cuts over his heart (the seventh scar), the prose slows to a crawl. We feel the steel. We feel Cassian's recognition that this wound is the one the prophecy predicted. Time stretches.
- When Cassian's sword enters Lucian below the ribs, the prose doesn't speed up for dramatic impact — it goes *quiet*. The noise drains out. The violence becomes intimate. Two brothers holding each other as one of them dies.
- Lucian reaching for Cassian's hand in his final moment — that's not choreography. That's a character beat. And it works because the pacing gave it space.

**Practical takeaway:** Read your fight scenes aloud. You'll hear where the rhythm flatlines. Alternate between fast external action and slow internal moments. Use short sentences for speed, longer ones for weight. Let silence do work.

## Lesson 4: The Aftermath Is Part of the Fight

Most writers end a fight scene at the moment of victory or defeat. That's like ending a song on the bridge. The resolution matters.

After Cassian defeats Lucian in the Challenge Right in Book 1, the scene doesn't end. We stay with Cassian as the arena goes quiet, as he makes his choice about mercy, as the political implications cascade through the crowd, as he walks away from a throne he just earned the right to claim.

After the Battle of Iron Bridge in Book 2, the scene doesn't end with victory. It ends with Cassian looking at the wound on his face — his sixth scar — and understanding that every time he tries to put the sword down, the world picks it up for him.

After the final duel, Cassian carries Lucian's body down the mountain. He could have left it. He could have let soldiers handle it. He carries his brother's corpse down a mountainside because that's who he is, even after his brother tried to kill him, even after he broke his own oath about mercy.

The aftermath is where meaning lives.

**Practical takeaway:** Budget at least 20-30% of your fight scene's word count for the aftermath. What does the character feel when the adrenaline fades? What does the victory cost? What does the world look like now that the violence is over? These are the moments readers remember.

## Lesson 5: Use the Environment as Character

Too many fight scenes happen in a void — a generic arena, an empty field, a nondescript room. The environment should be doing work.

The Grand Arena in Valdrath isn't just a setting — it's a character. It holds ten thousand people and five thousand years of history. When Cassian fights there, the ghosts of every previous Challenge Right are in the stands. The architecture itself argues for the old Code.

Greyport's streets during the dock bombing are cramped, industrial, full of workers and immigrants — people who have nothing to do with royal politics but suffer from it anyway. The environment forces you to reckon with the collateral damage of power struggles.

The Western Fortress where Cassian confronts Lucian for the last time is cold stone in the mountains, isolated, a place Lucian chose because he'd given up on belonging anywhere. The setting mirrors the character.

**Practical takeaway:** Before writing a fight, spend five minutes on the setting. What does this place mean to the characters? How does the terrain shape the tactics? What does the environment say about the thematic stakes? A duel in a church means something different from a duel in a gutter.

## Lesson 6: Let Your Characters Fight Differently

This seems obvious, but I see it violated constantly. If you swap character names in a fight scene and nothing changes, the scene is generic.

Cassian fights like a mechanic — efficient, precise, reading the machine of his opponent's body for weaknesses. He's clinical until emotion breaks through, and when it does, the shift is terrifying.

Lucian fights like someone who's been told his whole life that he doesn't belong. He's technically brilliant but emotionally volatile. His style is aggressive, pushing forward, always attacking — because defense feels like submission, and submission is what the world has been demanding from him since he learned the truth about his blood.

Vincent fights like the career soldier he is. Methodical, organized, treating combat as logistics. He doesn't seek single combat because that's ego — he seeks victory because that's duty.

Theo fights like someone still learning who he is. His technique is textbook in the early books, because all he has is training. By the later books, his style has evolved into something uniquely his — a blend of Cassian's precision and Marcus's strategic thinking, with a streak of reckless bravery that's entirely his own.

**Practical takeaway:** Write a paragraph describing how each major character fights — not their moves, but their *philosophy* of violence. Are they aggressive or reactive? Do they seek to dominate or to end the fight quickly? What does their fighting style reveal about their psychology? Then let those philosophies collide.

## The Rule That Governs All the Others

Here's the principle underneath everything I've said:

**A combat scene is a story compressed to its most urgent form.**

Every element of good storytelling — character, conflict, theme, setting, pacing, emotional stakes — exists inside a fight scene, just operating at higher pressure. If your fight scene doesn't have a beginning, middle, and end that change the characters involved, it's not a scene. It's a set piece.

Set pieces are fine for movies. In prose, we have access to something film doesn't: the inside of a character's head during the worst moments of their life. Use it.

Write fights that hurt. Write fights that change people. Write fights where the most important wound isn't the one that bleeds.

That's how you write combat scenes that matter.
