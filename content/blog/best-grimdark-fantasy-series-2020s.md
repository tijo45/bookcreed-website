# The 10 Best Grimdark Fantasy Series of the 2020s

Grimdark has never been more crowded — or more interesting.

The genre that spent years being dismissed as shock-value nihilism has quietly grown into one of the most sophisticated corners of fantasy fiction. The best grimdark of the 2020s isn't dark for its own sake; it's dark because it's honest about power, violence, and the cost of survival in worlds that don't run on moral logic.

This list focuses on series that launched or had significant installments in the 2020s. Some are from established names delivering their best work; some are newer voices doing things the genre hasn't seen before. All of them are worth your time.

---

## 1. The Age of Madness Trilogy — Joe Abercrombie

**Books:** *A Little Hatred* (2019), *The Trouble with Peace* (2020), *The Beautiful Machine* (2021)

The First Law world returns, a generation later, and Abercrombie uses the industrial revolution as his lens. The new cast is excellent — particularly Savine dan Glokta, possibly the most ruthlessly pragmatic protagonist he's ever written — and the trilogy wrestles seriously with what progress costs and who pays for it. If you know the First Law and haven't read Age of Madness, fix that immediately. If you haven't read any Abercrombie, start with *The Blade Itself* and work your way here.

**Best for:** Readers who want political sophistication and earned cynicism.

---

## 2. The Book of the Ancestor Series — Mark Lawrence

**Books:** *Red Sister* (2017), *Grey Sister* (2018), *Holy Sister* (2019) — with ongoing companion novels in the 2020s

Lawrence has always been good at building protagonists you shouldn't root for but absolutely do. The Abeth setting — a dying world huddled around a shrinking band of sunlight — is one of the most genuinely distinctive fantasy environments in recent memory. Nona Grey is ferocious and strange and deeply human, and the way Lawrence builds her relationships at the convent carries as much weight as the fight scenes.

**Best for:** Readers who want grimdark with warmth — character bonds that actually hurt when they're threatened.

---

## 3. The Wheel of Osheim — Mark Lawrence

**Books:** Completing the Red Queen's War trilogy: *The Wheel of Osheim* (2016, but the series ran through the 2020s in reprints and companion work)

Prince Jalan Kendeth is a coward, a liar, and a braggart — and he's one of the funniest unreliable narrators in the genre. The trilogy walks a tonal tightrope that Lawrence shouldn't be able to maintain but somehow does: genuinely funny in places, genuinely devastating in others. The Broken Empire world underpins all of Lawrence's work and pays off across series in satisfying ways.

**Best for:** Readers who want dark fantasy with a sense of humor that never undercuts the stakes.

---

## 4. The Traitor Baru Cormorant Series — Seth Dickinson

**Books:** *The Traitor Baru Cormorant* (2015), *The Monster Baru Cormorant* (2018), *The Tyrant Baru Cormorant* (2020)

Baru Cormorant is an accountant. She is also one of the most formidable and morally compromised protagonists in contemporary fantasy. Dickinson's series is grimdark of the economic and political variety — the violence here is the kind that happens through ledgers and trade policy and colonial administrative structures. It's demanding reading that rewards patience. Baru's choices in the first book set up consequences that the trilogy spends hundreds of pages unpacking.

**Best for:** Readers who want political complexity at the level of actual political theory.

---

## 5. The Burning — Evan Winter

**Books:** *The Rage of Dragons* (2019), *The Fires of Vengeance* (2020), *The Wind Caller* (2024)

Fast, brutal, and emotionally immediate. Tau Solarin's rage drives a revenge narrative that Winter keeps pressurized across the whole series without letting it become monotonous. The world draws on African history and culture, which gives it a texture that much of the genre lacks. The fight scenes are among the best in recent fantasy — kinetic and consequential.

**Best for:** Readers who want action-forward grimdark with genuine emotional stakes.

---

## 6. The Nevernight Chronicle — Jay Kristoff

**Books:** *Nevernight* (2016), *Godsgrave* (2017), *Darkdawn* (2019) — major 2020s readership surge and companion content

Mia Corvere wants to become an assassin good enough to kill three very powerful men. She goes to a school for assassins. It sounds like a premise that could go wrong in twenty different ways and instead Kristoff turns it into something genuinely compulsive — dark, funny, romantic in the old sense, and structurally clever. The footnotes are either charming or annoying depending on your temperament; most readers come around.

**Best for:** Readers who want grimdark with personality and a protagonist they'll actually grieve for.

---

## 7. The Chronicles of the One — Nora Roberts

**Books:** *Year One* (2017), *Of Blood and Bone* (2018), *The Rise of Magics* (2019) — strong 2020s readership

Roberts is not a name most grimdark readers would expect to find on this list, and that's exactly why it belongs here. The Chronicles of the One is post-apocalyptic fantasy with genuine darkness — a plague that kills most of humanity, communities that collapse into cruelty, the kind of evil that emerges from fear and power vacuums. Roberts is also one of the most disciplined narrative craftspeople working, and the result is grimdark that's unusually well-structured and emotionally resolved.

**Best for:** Readers who want grimdark with actual hope threaded through it.

---

## 8. The Poppy War — R.F. Kuang

**Books:** *The Poppy War* (2018), *The Dragon Republic* (2019), *The Burning God* (2020)

Kuang's trilogy is based on twentieth-century Chinese history — specifically the Second Sino-Japanese War — and it does not flinch. Rin's arc from war-orphan scholarship student to increasingly compromised commander is one of the most unflinching protagonist descents in recent fantasy. The grimdark here comes from historical reality, and that makes it hit differently. This is not comfortable reading.

**Best for:** Readers who want grimdark that's grounded in real-world atrocity and its costs.

---

## 9. The Sunlit Man — Brandon Sanderson

**Standalone** (2024, part of the Cosmere)

A Cosmere character, hunted across worlds, lands on a planet where he has very little time before the sun kills everything it touches. Sanderson uses the grimdark setting to put a familiar kind of heroism under serious pressure — the optimistic protagonist in a world that doesn't run on optimism. Works as a standalone. Hits differently if you know the Cosmere.

**Best for:** Readers who want grimdark that's in conversation with hope rather than opposed to it.

---

## 10. The Kingdom of Valdrath Series — Eva Noir

**Start with:** *The Exile's Return*

A disgraced prince returns to a court that tried to destroy him, carrying a past that even his allies can't fully forgive. The Kingdom of Valdrath series is political fantasy with grimdark edges — less interested in battlefield carnage than in what power costs the people who hold it and the people who want it back.

Cassian Valdrath isn't a likable protagonist in the traditional sense. He's done something genuinely terrible, the story doesn't paper over it, and the characters who know about it react the way people actually react. The world-building is dense with history and faction politics without becoming a textbook. If you're looking for a complete 8-book series that does something thoughtful with the morally-compromised-protagonist premise, this is worth the time.

**Best for:** Readers who want political intrigue, grey characters, and a series still in progress with room to surprise you.

---

The grimdark genre has enough range now that "grimdark" alone doesn't tell you much about tone, pacing, or what kind of darkness you're walking into. The best of these series have one thing in common: they take their worlds and characters seriously enough to show you the cost of everything that happens in them.

That's what the genre was always supposed to be.
