# 5 Fantasy Series to Read After The Warrior Prince Saga

Every time I finish a series I love, I feel that specific kind of empty — the one where you close the book and stare at the wall for a while, and then immediately start hunting for the next thing that'll make you feel that way again.

If you've just finished *The Warrior Prince Saga* (or you're between books and need something to tide you over), here are five series that scratch similar itches. These aren't clones of WPS — they're their own beasts. But they share DNA: morally complex characters, political systems that shape the story, combat that means something, and a refusal to give you easy answers about who deserves the throne.

## 1. The First Law Trilogy by Joe Abercrombie

**Start with:** *The Blade Itself*

If you loved Cassian's moral complexity, Joe Abercrombie is your next stop. The First Law trilogy features some of the most richly drawn morally grey characters in the genre — Logen Ninefingers, a barbarian trying to leave his violent past behind (sound familiar?), Glokta, a crippled torturer who's somehow the most compelling person in the room, and Jezal, a vain nobleman who gets the heroic journey he absolutely doesn't deserve.

**Why WPS fans will love it:** Abercrombie does something I deeply admire — he makes you root for people who've done terrible things, without ever letting you forget what they've done. If you connected with Cassian's guilt and his struggle to be better than his worst moment, Logen Ninefingers will get under your skin the same way. Plus, Abercrombie's fight scenes are phenomenal. Brutal, efficient, and always in service of character.

**Fair warning:** Abercrombie is darker than WPS in tone. Where my series argues that redemption is possible (even if it costs everything), Abercrombie is more skeptical. That's not a flaw — it's a feature. His cynicism is earned.

## 2. The Farseer Trilogy by Robin Hobb

**Start with:** *Assassin's Apprentice*

Robin Hobb's FitzChivalry Farseer is, in my opinion, one of the greatest character studies in all of fantasy. Fitz is a royal bastard trained as an assassin, caught between loyalty to the crown and his own desperate need for a life that belongs to him. The emotional depth is staggering.

**Why WPS fans will love it:** If the Cassian character study resonated — if you're drawn to protagonists who are defined more by what they endure than what they conquer — Hobb writes that better than almost anyone alive. The political intrigue of the Six Duchies echoes Valdrath's court politics, and the relationship between Fitz and his king has some of the same tension as Cassian and Daveth: loyalty to a ruler who demands too much, given by a person who's already lost too much.

**Fair warning:** Hobb writes at a slower pace than WPS. She's building something vast, and she takes her time. If you're patient — and if you let yourself sink into FitzChivalry's head — the payoff is extraordinary. The Farseer trilogy is the beginning of a sixteen-book saga (across multiple trilogies), and every one is worth reading.

## 3. Rage of Dragons by Evan Winter

**Start with:** *The Rage of Dragons*

This is the one for readers who came to WPS for the combat. Evan Winter writes some of the most visceral, propulsive fight scenes in modern fantasy. The story follows Tau, a young man from a lesser caste who dedicates himself to becoming the greatest swordsman in his society — not for glory, but for revenge.

**Why WPS fans will love it:** The combat traditions in Winter's world are as deeply embedded in the culture as the Trial of the Seven is in Valdrath. Fighting isn't just action — it's a social system, a hierarchy, a way of life. If you loved how WPS uses martial culture to explore questions about worth, power, and who gets to define them, *Rage of Dragons* is operating in the same space. Tau's relentless drive mirrors Cassian's intensity, but where Cassian is trying to put the sword down, Tau is trying to pick it up. The contrast is fascinating.

**Fair warning:** This is a revenge story at its core, and it's unrelenting. Tau's single-mindedness is the engine of the book, and it can be exhausting by design. But the worldbuilding — inspired by pre-colonial East African history — is fresh and extraordinary.

## 4. The Poppy War by R.F. Kuang

**Start with:** *The Poppy War*

R.F. Kuang's debut starts as a military academy story and then takes a hard left into something much darker and more ambitious. Rin, an orphan from the south, tests into the most elite military academy in the Nikara Empire. By the end of the first book, she's done things that make the Seven Farmers look like a misunderstanding.

**Why WPS fans will love it:** Kuang does something I tried to do with the Warrior Prince Saga — she explores what happens when power is given to people who've been brutalized by power. Rin's arc across three books is a masterclass in the corrupting nature of violence. If you're interested in how WPS handles the question "does the cycle of violence ever actually break?" Kuang's answer is more pessimistic and equally compelling.

The political systems are rich, the military strategy is meticulous, and the moral questions are genuinely difficult. Kuang doesn't tell you what to think about Rin. She shows you what Rin does and lets you wrestle with it.

**Fair warning:** This series gets *dark*. Kuang draws on real historical events (specifically the Second Sino-Japanese War and the Rape of Nanjing), and she doesn't flinch. It's powerful but heavy. Know what you're walking into.

## 5. The Broken Empire Trilogy by Mark Lawrence

**Start with:** *Prince of Thorns*

If Cassian represents a man trying to be better than his worst moment, Jorg Ancrath represents a teenager who *leans into it*. Prince of Thorns follows a fourteen-year-old prince leading a band of outlaws across a post-apocalyptic landscape that used to be Europe, and Jorg is one of the most polarizing protagonists in fantasy. You'll either be repulsed or fascinated. Probably both.

**Why WPS fans will love it:** Lawrence and I are interested in the same question from opposite angles: what does power do to the people who hold it? Cassian rejects power because he's seen what it costs. Jorg *pursues* it because he's decided the cost is worth paying. Reading them back-to-back creates a fascinating dialogue about kingship, violence, and whether a broken person can build something that isn't broken.

The prose is sharp, the pacing is relentless, and Lawrence has a gift for making you complicit in Jorg's choices. You'll catch yourself rooting for things you shouldn't, and that discomfort is the point.

**Fair warning:** Jorg does genuinely awful things, especially in the first book. Lawrence is deliberately testing your tolerance. If you can get through the first hundred pages, the character complexity deepens significantly — but those first hundred pages are a filter, and Lawrence knows it.

## The Common Thread

These five series share something fundamental with *The Warrior Prince Saga*: they refuse to separate violence from consequence. In each one, every sword swing, every political betrayal, every act of power leaves a mark — on the characters, on the world, on the reader.

That's the kind of fantasy I love reading, and it's the kind I write. Stories where the scars are the story.

If you've read any of these, I'd love to hear your thoughts. And if you haven't started *The Warrior Prince Saga* yet — well, [you know where to find it](https://bookcreed.com/series/warrior-prince-saga).

Happy reading.
